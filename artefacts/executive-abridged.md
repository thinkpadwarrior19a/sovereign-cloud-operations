# Part I — The Imperative

## Chapter 0 — Introduction: The Operational Gap

Every enterprise with a multi-cloud estate faces the same uncomfortable truth: the gap between what the architecture can theoretically deliver and what the organisation can reliably, safely, and compliantly operate is widening. Infrastructure spans three or four public clouds, several on-premises data centres, and an expanding edge. Monitoring tools proliferate. Compliance frameworks multiply. Engineering teams are stretched thin, spending more time navigating dashboards than resolving incidents.

This book — and this abridged edition — addresses that gap. The full argument rests on a single proposition: the operating model has not kept pace with the architecture it governs, and the only sustainable response is a *sovereign cloud operations control plane* that spans providers, respects jurisdictional boundaries, accommodates AI-driven agents, and remains genuinely explainable to both humans and regulators.

The patterns and practices that served organisations well in the early years of cloud adoption — dashboard-centric monitoring, ticket-based change management, periodic compliance reviews, siloed toolchains for each provider — are encountering structural limits. They were designed for a world that was simpler: fewer providers, less regulation, less data, less AI, smaller teams. The world has moved on. What has not kept pace is the operating model. Organisations have invested heavily in infrastructure — compute, storage, network, containerisation, automation tooling — but the layer that governs how those assets are managed, how decisions are made, how AI is used responsibly, and how sovereignty is maintained in practice has often lagged behind. The result is a growing gap between what the architecture can theoretically do and what the organisation can reliably and safely do with it.

The response to these pressures cannot be more dashboards, more analysts, or more compliance documentation. It must be a sovereign cloud operations control plane: a set of interconnected capabilities that continuously models the estate, correlates signals intelligently, surfaces recommended actions to human operators, executes approved changes through governed automation, and produces an auditable record of everything it does. IBM Concert, watsonx Orchestrate, IBM Instana, IBM Turbonomic, watsonx.governance and IBM Bob are the current industrial instantiation of many of these capabilities. This book uses them as concrete anchors for the patterns described, while maintaining enough abstraction that readers who use different tools can apply the concepts to their own environments. IBM's sovereign operations proposition is explicitly cross-cloud: the products described here are designed to operate as a control and intelligence layer above the hyperscaler estates, not as replacements for them.

The book is organised into twelve parts. Part I makes the strategic case for change. Parts II through IV establish the architectural reference model, observability architecture, and automation foundations. Parts V through IX cover IBM Concert, multi-agent orchestration, AI governance, agentic DevOps, and autonomous operations. Parts X and XI address the human operating model, maturity assessment, blueprints, and case studies. Part XII closes with the horizon — the fully agentic enterprise. This abridged edition distils the essential argument, architecture, and practical guidance from each part, providing a complete strategic overview in approximately one-tenth the length of the full text.

## Chapter 1 — The Limits of Yesterday's Operations

The first chapter articulates why the current operational state is unsustainable. The argument is structural, not a critique of any particular team or toolset. Dashboard-centric operations worked when the estate was small and the dependency graph was simple enough for an experienced engineer to hold in memory. A senior operator could glance at half a dozen dashboards, correlate a CPU spike with a recent deployment, and diagnose the problem in minutes. That era is over for any enterprise of meaningful scale.

The typical large enterprise now operates across two to four public cloud providers, multiple on-premises environments, and an increasingly distributed edge. Each provider has its own monitoring console, its own alerting language, its own identity model, and its own compliance artefacts. The result is what the book terms *operational fragmentation*: not a single estate with a unified control plane, but a collection of loosely connected estates, each with its own partial view. When an incident spans providers — a latency spike that originates in a network configuration change on one cloud and manifests as a timeout in an application hosted on another — the mean time to diagnosis is dominated not by the complexity of the fix but by the complexity of assembling context across toolchains. Engineers spend the first hour of a major incident not fixing anything but trying to construct a shared mental model of what is happening: copying links into chat, sharing screenshots, speculating about causes, discovering that multiple teams are investigating the same symptom independently from different angles. The cost of this fragmented correlation is measured not only in extended outage duration but in lost capacity for planned work, engineering burnout, and customer-visible impact.

Regulatory pressure compounds the problem. The EU's Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), which applies from January 2025 to financial entities across the EU, imposes specific obligations on ICT risk management, incident reporting within four hours for major incidents, digital operational resilience testing, and third-party provider oversight [1]. The NIS2 Directive extends similar obligations to essential and important entities across sixteen sectors, with penalties of up to EUR 10 million or two per cent of global turnover [2]. The EU AI Act introduces a risk-based classification of AI systems with mandatory conformity assessments for high-risk applications, including those used in critical infrastructure operations [3]. These are not aspirational guidelines; they are legal obligations with substantial penalties, supervisory enforcement, and board-level accountability. Management bodies face personal liability under NIS2 Article 20 for failures to comply with risk management obligations.

The talent dimension makes the problem harder still. Experienced cloud operations engineers are scarce and expensive. Organisations that consume their best engineers' time on repetitive correlation, manual compliance evidence assembly, and context-switching between provider consoles face attrition, burnout, and an ever-widening gap between the complexity of the estate and the capacity of the team to manage it. The book argues that this is not a problem that can be solved by hiring alone. The growth in operational complexity is exponential — driven by combinatorial interactions between services, providers, regions, and compliance frameworks — while headcount growth, at best, is linear.

The chapter concludes with the book's central design criterion: the sovereign cloud operations control plane must reduce the cognitive load on human operators by automating the assembly of context, the correlation of signals, the evaluation of compliance posture, and the execution of routine remediations — while preserving human authority over consequential decisions and producing a complete audit trail that satisfies the most demanding regulatory framework the organisation faces.

## Chapter 2 — The Economics of AI-Driven Operations

The economics chapter reframes cloud costs beyond infrastructure pricing and demonstrates why the business case for sovereign operations is compelling even before regulatory pressure is considered. Traditional cloud economics focused on unit prices: compute per hour, storage per gigabyte, data transfer per terabyte. Operations appeared as a line item — headcount, tool licences, overtime — rather than as a dominant cost driver. That framing systematically understated the true cost of operating a multi-cloud, sovereign estate.

The hidden costs fall into five categories. First, *incident exposure*: every hour of mean time to detect (MTTD) and mean time to resolve (MTTR) translates directly into revenue loss, customer impact, and reputational damage. Industry benchmarks place the cost of a major outage for a large enterprise at between USD 100,000 and USD 540,000 per hour, depending on the sector and the services affected [4]. When that incident spans multiple clouds and multiple teams must assemble context manually, the resolution time — and therefore the cost — inflates dramatically. A cross-cloud incident that takes four hours to resolve in a fragmented environment might take forty minutes in an environment with unified observability and correlation — the cost difference, for a revenue-critical service, can exceed USD 1 million per event.

Second, *toil burden*: the repetitive, manual, automatable work that consumes engineering time without producing lasting value. Google's SRE research established that high-performing organisations keep toil below fifty per cent of engineering time [5]; many enterprises, particularly those with fragmented multi-cloud estates, operate well above that threshold. Every hour spent manually rotating certificates, copying logs into ticketing systems, assembling compliance evidence from screenshots, or performing the same diagnostic sequence for a recurring incident is an hour of engineering talent consumed without lasting benefit.

Third, *regulatory fine risk*: DORA penalties can reach the higher of EUR 5 million or a percentage of revenue for financial entities, while NIS2 penalties reach EUR 10 million or two per cent of global turnover for essential entities. These are not theoretical — the first enforcement actions under DORA's predecessor frameworks have already been levied, and supervisory authorities across the EU have signalled that enforcement of the new frameworks will be vigorous from their application dates.

Fourth, *talent attrition*: engineers who spend their days on repetitive, low-value operational work leave. Replacing a senior cloud engineer costs between 1.5 and 2 times their annual compensation when recruitment, onboarding, and lost productivity are factored in. The organisations best positioned to retain talent are those that can offer engineers meaningful, challenging work — which requires freeing them from the operational drudgery that currently consumes their time.

Fifth, *opportunity cost*: every hour spent firefighting is an hour not spent on resilience improvement, feature development, or architectural modernisation. The Forsgren, Humble, and Kim research underlying the DORA programme demonstrates that high-performing engineering organisations are not merely more efficient; they are structurally more capable of responding to market demands [6]. They can release new features faster, experiment more cheaply, and recover from mistakes more quickly. Organisations mired in reactive operations find that the demand for firefighting consistently crowds out investment in improvement. The product roadmap is hostage to the operations backlog.

The chapter introduces five value metrics that connect operational investment to business outcomes: *cost per incident* (total cost including engineering time, revenue impact, and regulatory consequences divided by incident count), *mean time to context* (how quickly an operator reaches a comprehensive understanding of an incident's scope and likely cause), *automation coverage ratio* (the proportion of operational tasks handled without manual intervention), *compliance evidence latency* (the time required to produce audit-ready evidence for a given control), and *engineering capacity allocation* (the split between reactive firefighting and proactive improvement work). Organisations that invest in a unified sovereign control plane consistently improve all five metrics.

The business case for agentic operations is not primarily about headcount reduction. It is about the reallocation of expensive human expertise from low-value, repetitive work — correlation, evidence assembly, routine remediation — to high-value work that only humans can do: architectural design, boundary-setting, governance, and judgement in novel situations. The economic return is measured not in fewer engineers but in more capable engineering, faster recovery, lower fine exposure, and reduced attrition.

## Chapter 3 — Regulatory Landscape and Design Criteria

The regulatory chapter surveys the compliance frameworks that shape operational architecture for enterprises in regulated jurisdictions, and derives from them a set of concrete operational design criteria. This is not a legal treatise; it is an architect's reading of what the regulations demand in operational terms.

DORA (Regulation (EU) 2022/2554) applies to financial entities across the EU and imposes five pillars of obligation: ICT risk management (Articles 5-16), requiring comprehensive risk management frameworks with continuous identification, protection, detection, response, and recovery capabilities; ICT-related incident management and reporting (Articles 17-23), including mandatory classification of incidents using defined criteria and reporting of major incidents to competent authorities within four hours of classification, with intermediate and final reports within specified timeframes; digital operational resilience testing (Articles 24-27), including advanced threat-led penetration testing at least every three years for significant institutions; ICT third-party risk management (Articles 28-44), including an oversight framework for critical third-party providers and mandatory contractual provisions covering data location, access, security, and exit strategies; and information sharing (Article 45) [1]. Article 12 specifically addresses backup policies and restoration procedures, requiring financial entities to maintain backup systems with sufficient geographic diversity — a requirement that directly informs sovereign zone design and cyber-resilient recovery architecture.

NIS2 (Directive (EU) 2022/2555) extends cybersecurity obligations to essential and important entities across sixteen sectors, including energy, transport, banking, health, water, digital infrastructure, and public administration [2]. It requires risk management measures encompassing incident handling, business continuity, supply chain security, network security, and vulnerability disclosure (Article 21); incident notification within twenty-four hours of becoming aware of a significant incident, with a full incident report within seventy-two hours (Article 23); and governance measures ensuring that management bodies approve and oversee cybersecurity risk management (Article 20). Penalties reach EUR 10 million or two per cent of global turnover for essential entities, and EUR 7 million or 1.4 per cent for important entities. Management bodies can be held personally liable for failures to discharge their oversight obligations.

The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) classifies AI systems by risk level — unacceptable, high-risk, limited, and minimal — with mandatory obligations for high-risk systems [3]. AI systems used in critical infrastructure management, including those providing operational decision support and automated remediation, may fall within the high-risk category. High-risk systems must comply with Article 9 (risk management system throughout the lifecycle), Article 10 (data and data governance), Article 11 (technical documentation), Article 12 (record-keeping enabling monitoring of operation), Article 13 (transparency to deployers), Article 14 (human oversight measures), and Article 72 (post-market monitoring). These requirements have direct implications for the design of agentic operations: every operational AI agent must be documented, governed, monitored, and subject to human oversight in a manner that satisfies these provisions.

From these frameworks and others — GDPR (Article 22 on automated decision-making, Articles 44-49 on international data transfers), sector-specific prudential requirements, national implementations — the book derives eight operational design criteria: continuous compliance evidence generation, jurisdiction-aware data handling, auditable decision trails for all consequential actions, human oversight for AI-assisted operations, rapid incident detection and reporting capability, resilience testing infrastructure, third-party risk visibility, and sovereign zone enforcement. These criteria inform every architectural decision in the chapters that follow.

# Part II — The Architectural Reference Model

## Chapter 4 — The Four-Planes Reference Model

The architectural heart of the book is a four-plane reference model that gives every concern in sovereign cloud operations a clear architectural home. The model extends the zero-copy integration framework — where data is accessed at the source rather than copied into silos, state changes propagate as events, and data flows are governed through a unified control plane — with an operations-specific decomposition. The decomposition is consistent with TOGAF's principle of separating stakeholder concerns into coherent domains, though it applies that principle specifically to sovereign cloud operations rather than to enterprise architecture in the general sense.

The **Observability Plane** is the perceptual layer. It continuously collects, correlates, and contextualises signals from across the estate: metrics, events, logs, traces, and topology. IBM Instana provides automatic discovery of services, processes, and infrastructure components, deploying lightweight agents on every host and container that build a dependency graph updating in real time without manual configuration. Instana ingests metrics at one-second granularity, traces every request end-to-end through the distributed system, collects structured and unstructured logs, and tracks infrastructure and application events. The AutoProfile capability continuously profiles application code in production, identifying performance hotspots without traditional profiling overhead. IBM Turbonomic adds resource-level intelligence, modelling the supply and demand of compute, memory, storage, and network resources to generate placement and scaling recommendations that optimise both performance and cost within sovereign zone constraints. Critically, the observability plane must be sovereignty-aware: raw telemetry remains within sovereign zone boundaries, with only filtered aggregates exported to a central resilience view, satisfying the tension between DORA's demand for comprehensive evidence and GDPR's data minimisation requirements.

The **Automation and Orchestration Plane** translates decisions into governed actions. Infrastructure as code (Terraform, Ansible) defines the desired state of every resource. GitOps workflows ensure that every change is version-controlled, peer-reviewed, and auditable. Ansible Automation Platform provides the execution engine for operational runbooks, with role-based access control, credential management, and execution logging that satisfies the audit trail requirements of DORA Article 12 and NIS2 Article 21. This plane does not act on its own initiative; it executes actions that have been proposed by the intelligence plane, authorised by the governance plane, and approved (where required) by a human operator. Every action is codified, version-controlled, tested, and subject to the same governance as application code.

The **Agentic Intelligence Plane** provides the reasoning capability that bridges observation to action. IBM Concert sits at the centre of this plane, continuously correlating signals from the observability plane, mapping them against a typed entity graph of the entire estate, and generating prioritised, evidence-backed recommendations. Concert's correlation engine combines graph-based reasoning (following dependency paths to identify likely causes), temporal correlation (identifying co-occurring changes and anomalies), and historical pattern matching (comparing current signal patterns to previously observed incidents) to perform causal inference. watsonx Orchestrate provides the conversational interface through which operators interact with agents and through which multi-step workflows are composed from atomic actions. Specialised agents — incident diagnosis agents, capacity planning agents, compliance assessment agents, change risk agents — operate within the intelligence plane, each with defined competencies, defined boundaries, and defined escalation paths.

The **Governance and Audit Plane** provides the accountability infrastructure that makes the other three planes trustworthy. Policy-as-code definitions, expressed in Open Policy Agent's Rego language and enforced through Kubernetes admission controllers, Terraform plan evaluators (Conftest), and workflow gates, ensure that governance is a continuous, embedded constraint rather than a periodic audit exercise. watsonx.governance maintains the model inventory, approval records, and performance monitoring for every AI model and agent in the estate. The sovereign AI record — an immutable, structured log of every consequential agent action — satisfies the evidentiary requirements of DORA, the EU AI Act, and sector-specific frameworks simultaneously.

The model addresses the meta-lock-in concern directly. Each plane's components are substitutable because the interfaces between planes are defined by open standards — OpenTelemetry for observability, Kubernetes and Terraform for automation, OPA for policy, OpenAPI for integration. The architecture's value lies in the decomposition and the interface contracts, not in any single vendor's implementation. A dedicated section in the full text addresses the "who watches the watchmen" question — whether the cross-cloud control plane itself becomes a single point of compromise — and describes the distributed trust architecture, zone-scoped credentials, just-in-time privilege elevation, and zone-local enforcement mechanisms that bound the blast radius of any control plane breach.

## Chapter 5 — Multi-Cloud Topology and Sovereign Zones

A sovereign zone is a unit of operational, jurisdictional, and compliance governance within the multi-cloud estate. Each zone has defined boundaries — typically aligned to a legal jurisdiction such as the EU, the UK, or a specific member state — and a set of constraints that apply to all resources within it: data residency requirements, encryption standards, identity and access policies, network isolation rules, and regulatory framework mappings.

Sovereign zones are not merely labels applied to cloud provider regions; they are technically enforced boundaries. Each zone is implemented as a set of infrastructure resources — cloud accounts, virtual private clouds, Kubernetes clusters, identity providers, observability backends — with explicit network boundaries and policy-as-code enforcement that prevents workload deployment outside the designated zone without explicit exception approval. Zone boundaries are encoded in infrastructure as code using Terraform modules and enforced through OPA policies that evaluate every deployment, configuration change, and data flow against zone constraints. Drift detection continuously validates that actual state matches declared zone policy, and violations are surfaced within minutes.

The architecture supports hierarchical zone structures. A parent zone defines baseline constraints that child zones inherit and may tighten but not loosen. A European banking group might define an EU parent zone with GDPR and DORA baseline requirements, then child zones for Germany, France, and the Netherlands, each adding BaFin, ACPR, or DNB-specific controls respectively. A UK zone would inherit different baseline constraints reflecting UK GDPR and PRA/FCA supervisory requirements. IBM Satellite provides the deployment substrate for extending consistent operational governance across hyperscaler regions, on-premises data centres, and edge locations, managing the lifecycle of OpenShift clusters and associated resources within defined zone boundaries.

Zone-to-zone interactions are governed by explicit inter-zone policies. Data flows between zones must satisfy the constraints of both the source and destination zones. For regulated data, cross-zone flows may require anonymisation, aggregation, or pseudonymisation at the zone boundary. Network policies enforce zone isolation at the infrastructure level, with inter-zone traffic routed through defined gateway points where policy evaluation occurs. The topology model maintained by Concert includes zone membership as a first-class attribute of every entity, enabling zone-aware reasoning about the impact of operational actions on cross-zone dependencies.

## Chapter 6 — Zero-Copy Integration as Operational Substrate

Zero-copy integration is not merely a data architecture pattern; it is a sovereignty enabler. The traditional approach to multi-system integration involved copying data between systems — nightly batch extracts, ETL pipelines, replicated databases — creating multiple copies of sensitive data scattered across the estate. Each copy required its own access controls, encryption, data residency enforcement, and compliance documentation. In a multi-jurisdictional environment, this multiplication of copies creates an unmanageable governance burden. The European banking group case study (Chapter 36) identified over 340 replicated datasets across its subsidiaries, each representing a data residency risk and a compliance liability. Each of those copies was a potential GDPR violation, a potential data breach vector, and a line item on the audit schedule.

The zero-copy model replaces copies with governed access. Core systems expose APIs and event streams. Downstream consumers subscribe to events and maintain materialised views rather than owning copies of raw data. The event mesh — typically implemented on Apache Kafka with zone-aware topic routing — ensures that events containing regulated data remain within sovereign zone boundaries while filtered aggregates flow cross-zone for operational intelligence and reporting. Consumers that previously owned copies of raw transaction tables — risk calculation engines, anti-money laundering systems, marketing analytics, regulatory reporting — are migrated to subscribing to event streams and maintaining lightweight projections. The ownership model is inverted: the data remains with its authoritative source, and consumers receive what they need, when they need it, through governed channels.

This pattern has direct operational consequences. It reduces the number of data stores that must be audited for compliance — in the banking case study, from 340 copies to 41 managed exceptions. It eliminates the category of "stale copy" failures — incidents caused by consumers operating on data that has drifted from the source, which in the healthcare case study had produced silent reconciliation failures between the national EHR and regional hospital systems that went undetected for days. It enables the observability plane to trace data lineage from source through transformation to consumption, providing the provenance information that regulators increasingly require under DORA's ICT risk management framework. And it makes sovereign zone enforcement tractable: rather than governing hundreds of data copies across dozens of systems, the organisation governs a defined set of event streams and API endpoints with clear ownership and policy attachment.

For operational telemetry specifically, zero-copy integration means that raw observability data — metrics, logs, traces — remains in its originating sovereign zone while the agentic intelligence plane operates on aggregated, filtered views. This is the architectural pattern that enables Concert to provide cross-zone operational intelligence without violating data residency constraints. The pattern also applies to the sovereign AI record: agent action logs remain in the zone where the action occurred, and cross-zone governance queries return only the aggregated, anonymised views that zone policy permits.

# Part III — Observability and Signals

## Chapter 7 — Sovereignty-Aware Observability Architecture

Observability in a sovereign estate must move beyond the traditional three-pillar model of metrics, logs, and traces. The book extends this to a five-signal model: metrics, events, logs, traces, and topology. Events — change events, deployment events, scaling events, policy evaluation events — are the connective tissue that links infrastructure changes to operational outcomes. Topology — the continuously updated map of what exists and how it relates — is the contextual layer that transforms raw signals into situated understanding. A metric that says "response time increased by 200ms" is information; a metric correlated with a topology model that says "response time increased by 200ms on a service that depends on a database that was patched fifteen minutes ago, in a sovereign zone where that database serves three other critical services" is actionable intelligence.

IBM Instana implements this five-signal model through automatic discovery and instrumentation. Instana agents, deployed on every host and container in the estate, automatically discover running processes, services, and their dependencies without requiring manual configuration or service map maintenance. Instana ingests metrics at one-second granularity — significantly higher resolution than the typical sixty-second collection interval of traditional monitoring — enabling detection of transient anomalies that would be invisible at lower resolution. Every request is traced end-to-end through the distributed system, with automatic context propagation across service boundaries. Structured and unstructured logs are collected and correlated with traces and metrics, enabling an operator to move from a metric anomaly to the specific trace, the specific log entry, and the specific code path without switching tools. Instana's AutoProfile capability continuously profiles application code in production, identifying performance hotspots — the specific methods and call paths consuming the most CPU or memory — without requiring developers to instrument their code or accept the overhead of traditional profiling tools.

The observability architecture must be sovereignty-aware in its data handling. Telemetry is regulated data: logs may contain personal information, traces may reveal business logic, metrics correlated with user activity may constitute processing under GDPR. The federated observability pattern addresses this by keeping zone-local observability backends — Instana tenants or on-premises Instana backends — within each sovereign zone, retaining raw telemetry at full fidelity within jurisdictional boundaries. Only filtered aggregates, scrubbed of personal data through field-level masking and log scrubbing pipelines, are exported to a central resilience view. This design satisfies DORA's demand for comprehensive operational evidence (the zone-local backend retains everything) while respecting GDPR's data minimisation principle and the data residency constraints of each zone.

OpenTelemetry provides the standardisation layer. The OpenTelemetry SDK, protocol (OTLP), and semantic conventions ensure that telemetry data is collected in a vendor-neutral, interoperable format. This decouples the instrumentation investment from the backend choice: organisations instrument with OpenTelemetry and route signals to Instana today, while retaining the option to route to alternative backends in the future. The W3C Trace Context standard ensures that distributed traces propagate context across service boundaries, including across sovereign zone boundaries, enabling end-to-end visibility without requiring raw data to leave its originating zone.

The chapter also addresses observability of AI components. Model invocations, agent decisions, confidence scores, and token consumption are first-class telemetry signals that feed both operational diagnosis and the sovereign AI record required by the EU AI Act. When an agent recommends a remediation, the observability plane captures what model was invoked, what context was provided, what confidence score was returned, and how long the inference took — information essential for both operational troubleshooting and regulatory compliance with Article 12 record-keeping requirements.

## Chapters 8–9 — Network Observability, Events, and Lineage

Network observability adds a critical layer for sovereign estates where data must not traverse prohibited paths. Flow-level visibility — knowing not just that packets moved between two endpoints but what route they took, whether that route crossed a sovereign zone boundary, and whether the transit complied with network policy — is essential for demonstrating compliance with data residency requirements. In the banking case study, network observability was instrumental in validating that the zone-aware Kafka topic routing was functioning correctly: network flow data confirmed that events containing customer-identifiable data were not traversing zone boundaries, providing a second layer of evidence beyond the application-level controls. The chapter details how network observability integrates with the topology model to provide continuous validation that data flows respect sovereign boundaries, and how network performance data feeds Concert's correlation engine for incidents involving latency, packet loss, or route changes.

Events and lineage form the fourth and fifth observability signals. Change events — deployments, configuration changes, scaling actions, certificate rotations, policy updates — are correlated with performance and availability signals to provide the change intelligence that Chapter 15 covers in depth. When a service degrades after a deployment, the ability to automatically correlate the degradation with the specific deployment, the specific configuration delta, and the specific topology impact is the difference between a twenty-minute diagnosis and a four-hour investigation. Data lineage — the provenance of data from its source through transformations to its consumption points — provides the traceability that regulators require for regulated data and that the EU AI Act requires for training data used in high-risk AI systems. In the observability context, lineage also tracks the provenance of operational signals: which raw signals contributed to a correlated situation, which situations contributed to a recommendation, and which recommendations led to actions — a chain of evidence that the sovereign AI record captures in full.

## Chapter 10 — Continuous Compliance Monitoring

The compliance chapter reframes compliance as a continuous operational property rather than a periodic audit exercise. The traditional model — regulation arrives, programme launches, controls are documented, auditors review a snapshot every twelve months — is fundamentally incompatible with an estate that changes hundreds of times per day. Between audits, the compliance posture can drift materially: configurations change, new services are deployed, access permissions are modified, network routes are altered. The audit snapshot may be accurate on the day it was taken and misleading by the following week.

Continuous compliance monitoring replaces the snapshot with a stream. The compliance signal fabric continuously evaluates the estate against the policy-as-code corpus from four signal classes. Configuration signals from cloud provider APIs, Kubernetes cluster state, and infrastructure-as-code definitions detect deviations from declared baselines — an S3 bucket with public access enabled in a sovereign zone that requires private access, a container image pulled from an unapproved registry, an encryption key that does not meet the zone's minimum key length requirement. Access signals from identity providers, cloud provider audit logs, and Kubernetes RBAC configurations detect privilege escalations, anomalous access patterns, and violations of least-privilege policies. Network signals from VPC flow logs, firewall rule evaluations, and service mesh telemetry detect data flows that cross sovereign zone boundaries without authorisation. Change signals from deployment pipelines, GitOps reconciliation, and configuration management detect changes that were not approved through the defined change management process.

IBM Concert aggregates these signals into a unified compliance posture view, mapping technical control evaluations to regulatory framework requirements. Each technical finding — a misconfigured encryption setting, an overly permissive IAM role, a network route that bypasses a zone gateway — is mapped to the specific DORA article, ISO/IEC 27001 control, NIST SP 800-53 control, or sector-specific requirement that it satisfies or violates. This regulatory mapping provides a clear line from technical posture to legal obligation. IBM OpenPages serves as the governance system of record, maintaining the control library, the risk register, and the evidence repository that regulators and auditors examine.

Policy-as-code is the technical foundation. Open Policy Agent (OPA), with policies written in the Rego language, evaluates configuration and access decisions against defined rules. Gatekeeper extends OPA to Kubernetes admission control, preventing non-compliant resources from being created — a deployment that violates zone constraints is rejected at admission, never reaching the cluster. Conftest evaluates Terraform plans before infrastructure is provisioned, catching sovereignty violations before they reach the cloud provider. Chef InSpec automates CIS Benchmark evaluation, verifying that operating system, database, and middleware configurations meet hardening standards.

The compliance automation delivers three measurable benefits. First, *evidence latency reduction*: the time to produce audit-ready evidence drops from weeks of manual assembly to minutes of report generation from continuously maintained data. In the banking case study, annual audit preparation effort reduced by an estimated 2,100 person-hours. Second, *drift detection*: policy violations are detected within minutes of occurrence rather than at the next scheduled audit, enabling rapid remediation before a violation becomes a finding. Third, *regulatory mapping*: each technical control evaluation is mapped to the specific regulatory provisions it satisfies, providing auditors with a direct connection between operational evidence and legal requirements.

Agentic compliance workflows extend this further. When drift is detected — a service deployed in a sovereign zone with an external dependency that violates zone policy, for example — a compliance agent automatically raises a remediation ticket, cites the specific policy clause, proposes remediation paths (move the service to the development zone where the dependency is permitted, or submit the dependency for sovereign compliance review), and, within defined autonomy boundaries, executes the approved remediation. The human role shifts from discovering violations and assembling evidence to reviewing agent assessments and exercising judgement about remediation priorities.

# Part IV — Automation and Infrastructure as Code

## Chapter 11 — Infrastructure as Code as the Spine

Infrastructure as code is not merely a deployment convenience; it is the prerequisite for every other capability in the sovereign operations stack. You cannot enforce sovereign zone constraints through policy-as-code if the infrastructure is not defined in code. You cannot detect configuration drift if there is no declared desired state to drift from. You cannot produce auditable change records if changes are made through cloud provider consoles. You cannot enable agents to propose and execute infrastructure changes if those changes are not expressible as code. The maturity model (Chapter 33) makes this dependency explicit: sovereignty enforcement at Level 3 and above requires infrastructure codification at Level 3 and above.

The chapter establishes IaC as the spine of sovereign operations: the structural element that everything else depends upon. Terraform provides the declarative infrastructure layer, defining resources across multiple cloud providers in a consistent language. Terraform modules encode sovereign zone boundaries, ensuring that resources are provisioned only in approved regions and accounts. Module composition enforces zone inheritance: a child zone module inherits its parent's constraints and can add but not remove restrictions. Ansible provides the imperative configuration and orchestration layer, executing operational procedures with idempotent, testable playbooks. GitOps workflows — where the git repository is the single source of truth and reconciliation controllers (such as Argo CD or Flux) ensure that actual state matches declared state — provide the change management model that satisfies auditability requirements.

For sovereign estates, IaC must itself be sovereignty-aware. Terraform state files — which contain sensitive information about the infrastructure including resource identifiers, IP addresses, and configuration parameters — must respect data residency requirements, stored in zone-local backends with appropriate encryption and access controls. The separation of state by sovereign zone ensures that infrastructure metadata for resources in the German zone, for example, is stored within German-jurisdiction backends and is not accessible from other zones without explicit, policy-evaluated authorisation.

## Chapter 12 — Runbooks, Automation, and Operational Memory

Codified runbooks are the operational muscle memory of the sovereign estate. Where IaC defines what the estate should look like, runbooks define how it should be operated: how incidents are diagnosed, how services are restarted, how certificates are rotated, how backups are verified, how capacity is adjusted. Ansible playbooks, version-controlled alongside the services they support, encode these procedures as executable, testable, auditable automation. Ansible Automation Platform provides the execution engine, offering a centralised inventory, role-based access control that respects sovereign zone boundaries, credential management that integrates with zone-local vaults, and comprehensive execution logging that produces the audit trail required by DORA and NIS2.

The chapter positions runbooks on a maturity continuum from manual procedures documented in wikis (error-prone, inconsistent, unauditable), through semi-automated workflows triggered by operators (consistent execution, human-gated), to fully autonomous execution within defined policy boundaries (agent-triggered, policy-evaluated, logged, and subject to automatic rollback). At each level, the essential requirements are the same: the runbook must be version-controlled, it must be tested before production use, it must produce an execution log, and it must be subject to the same governance as any other change to the production estate. Testing is particularly important: runbooks that are not tested against realistic conditions will fail in production at the worst possible time — during an incident, when reliability matters most.

The distinction between runbooks and ad-hoc scripts is governance. A script that an engineer wrote during an incident and saved to their home directory is not a runbook; it is technical debt. A runbook is reviewed, tested, integrated into the automation platform, and maintained as the service it supports evolves. The automation library should be structured around operational domains — certificate management, capacity adjustment, backup verification, network troubleshooting, security response — with clear ownership and regular testing schedules.

In the fully agentic enterprise, runbooks become the action vocabulary that agents draw upon: Concert identifies a situation, the intelligence plane selects the appropriate runbook, the governance plane evaluates whether execution is authorised, and the automation plane executes it — with the entire chain recorded in the sovereign AI record. The runbook library is not static; it evolves as agents identify patterns in the incidents they handle and as post-incident reviews reveal gaps in the existing automation. At maturity Level 5, runbooks are refined automatically based on execution outcome data, with human review for significant changes.

## Chapter 13 — Secrets, Identity, and Access

Secrets management and identity federation across a multi-cloud, multi-zone estate present unique challenges that are often underestimated until they become operational emergencies — a certificate expiration that takes down a production service, a leaked credential that triggers an incident response, a privilege escalation that violates a sovereign zone boundary. Each cloud provider has its own secrets management service (AWS Secrets Manager, Azure Key Vault, Google Secret Manager, IBM Secrets Manager), its own identity model, and its own access control mechanisms. Sovereign zone boundaries add constraints: credentials for resources within a zone must be managed within that zone, with zone-local vaults and zone-local identity providers; cross-zone access requires explicit policy authorisation and just-in-time privilege elevation rather than standing credentials.

The chapter addresses vault federation patterns — where each zone maintains its own secrets vault and cross-zone secret access is mediated through a policy-evaluated federation layer that logs every access, every use, and every rotation. Certificate lifecycle management is automated through zone-aware certificate authorities with automatic renewal, propagation verification (the lack of which caused the incident in the Chapter 38 day-in-the-life narrative), and expiration alerting.

For agentic operations, identity management extends to agents: each agent has a defined identity, defined credentials scoped to its sovereign zone, defined privilege boundaries, and an audit trail of every credential use. The principle of least privilege is technically enforced through zone-scoped service accounts, short-lived tokens (measured in minutes, not days), and just-in-time elevation for specific actions rather than broad standing permissions. An incident diagnosis agent receives read-only access to telemetry within its zone; a remediation agent receives time-bounded, action-specific credentials that are revoked immediately after execution completes. This credential discipline is essential for containing the blast radius of any single compromised agent.

# Part V — IBM Concert: The Operational Brain

## Chapter 14 — Concert Architecture and Core Concepts

IBM Concert functions as the operational brain of the sovereign estate — the system that maintains a continuously updated model of what exists, correlates signals to understand what is happening, and generates prioritised recommendations for what should be done. The chapter describes Concert's architecture through three core capabilities: topology discovery, signal correlation, and recommendation generation.

**Topology discovery** is Concert's foundation. Concert continuously builds and maintains a typed entity graph — a structured model of services, deployments, infrastructure components, databases, message queues, load balancers, cloud accounts, and sovereign zones, together with the relationships between them. Discovery sources include Instana's automatic service discovery (which provides the most granular, real-time view of running services and their dependencies), cloud provider APIs (which provide account-level resource inventory), Kubernetes cluster state (which provides workload deployment information), CMDB imports (which provide organisational context and ownership data), Terraform state files (which provide the declared desired state), and manual declarations for components that resist automatic discovery such as legacy mainframe services or third-party SaaS dependencies. The entity graph is not a static CMDB; it is a continuously updated, versioned model that reflects the actual state of the estate in near-real time. When a new container is deployed, Concert knows about it within seconds. When a dependency changes, Concert updates the graph and re-evaluates the health and risk posture of affected services.

Each entity carries typed metadata: its sovereign zone membership, its data classification, its compliance framework mappings, its SLO definitions, its owning team, and its change history. Relationships are also typed: "depends on," "deployed on," "routes through," "owned by," "governed by." This rich typing enables Concert to answer questions that flat monitoring tools cannot: "which business services are affected by this database degradation?" or "which sovereign zone policies apply to this proposed change?" or "what is the blast radius if this Kubernetes node fails?"

**Signal correlation** is Concert's reasoning engine. Signals from the observability plane are ingested and mapped against the entity graph. Concert's correlation engine combines three approaches: graph-based reasoning (following dependency paths to identify likely causes — if a database is degraded and three services depend on it, the database is the probable root cause of all three services' symptoms), temporal correlation (identifying co-occurring changes and anomalies within a defined time window), and historical pattern matching (comparing current signal patterns to previously observed and resolved incidents). The result is a causal inference with a confidence score: not merely "these signals are correlated" but "this metric degradation is probably caused by this configuration change, based on the dependency path and the temporal relationship."

**Recommendation generation** converts causal inferences into actionable guidance. Each recommendation is a structured object containing: the situation assessment, the proposed action, the evidence supporting the recommendation, the risk assessment (including blast radius and reversibility), the sovereign zone constraints that apply, and the approval requirements defined by the governance plane. Recommendations are scored by priority — combining business impact, severity, and time sensitivity — and presented to operators through Concert's interface or routed to watsonx Orchestrate for conversational interaction. Concert can also route recommendations directly to automation agents for execution within defined autonomy boundaries, producing a closed-loop remediation cycle for well-understood failure modes.

Concert's sovereign zone awareness is architecturally fundamental. Concert maintains zone boundaries as first-class entities in its graph and enforces them in recommendation logic. A recommendation to fail over a database to a different region will not be generated if the target region falls outside the service's sovereign zone. In federated deployments — where each sovereign zone has its own Concert instance with a lightweight coordination layer — zone-local recommendations are generated locally with full access to local telemetry, while cross-zone recommendations are generated at the coordination layer using only the filtered aggregates that zone policy permits to be exported.

# Part VI — Multi-Agent Orchestration and ITSM

## Chapter 18 — Multi-Agent Orchestration Patterns

A single general-purpose agent is insufficient at enterprise scale. The complexity and diversity of operational tasks — incident diagnosis, capacity planning, compliance assessment, change risk evaluation, knowledge management — requires specialised agents, each with deep competence in a defined domain. The chapter identifies five agent roles that recur across operational workflows: **planners** that decompose complex tasks into sub-tasks and determine sequencing; **executors** that perform specific technical actions (running diagnostics, applying configurations, executing runbooks); **reviewers** that validate the outputs of other agents against quality criteria, policy constraints, and factual accuracy; **guardrail agents** that evaluate proposed actions against policy-as-code definitions and sovereign zone constraints; and **synthesisers** that aggregate the outputs of multiple agents into coherent recommendations or reports for human consumption.

The chapter examines three orchestration topologies. The **centralised supervisor** pattern uses a planner agent that decomposes tasks, assigns sub-tasks to specialist agents, and aggregates their results. This is most suitable for structured workflows such as incident response, where the sequence is relatively predictable. The **peer-to-peer mesh** allows agents to communicate directly, sharing findings and requesting assistance without a central coordinator — more suitable for exploratory tasks such as root cause analysis across multiple domains. The **hierarchical** pattern combines both, with supervisors at each level and peer communication within levels.

Communication between agents uses durable message queues rather than synchronous API calls. This ensures that agent interactions are reliable, auditable, and recoverable — if an agent fails mid-workflow, the queue preserves the state and another instance can resume. Shared context is provided through Concert's entity graph and vector-database retrieval augmented generation (RAG), giving every agent in the pipeline access to the same topology model and operational knowledge base.

The chapter catalogues five principal failure modes and their mitigations. **Cascading hallucination** — one agent's erroneous output becoming another's input — is mitigated by independent validation gates between stages. **Scope creep** — an agent exceeding its defined competency — is mitigated by explicit scope boundaries enforced through OPA policies. **Coordination deadlock** — agents waiting for each other in a cycle — is mitigated by timeout policies and detection. **Conflicting actions** — two agents independently proposing incompatible remediations — is mitigated by a conflict resolution stage that evaluates proposals against the topology model. **Audit gaps** — unlogged agent-to-agent interactions — are mitigated by mandatory logging of every message, decision, and action in the sovereign AI record.

## Chapter 20 — Knowledge-Augmented Operations

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) connects agents to the organisation's accumulated operational knowledge: runbooks, incident post-mortems, architecture documentation, vendor knowledge bases, and regulatory guidance. Rather than relying solely on the foundation model's training data — which may be generic, outdated, or hallucinated — agents retrieve relevant knowledge from curated, indexed, vector-database sources and incorporate it into their reasoning. This grounds agent responses in verified, organisation-specific knowledge and significantly reduces hallucination risk. When an incident diagnosis agent encounters a database connection timeout, it retrieves not only the generic runbook for connection timeouts but also the post-mortem from the last three similar incidents in this organisation, including the specific environmental factors and remediation steps that proved effective.

The knowledge management architecture includes version-controlled knowledge assets (runbooks, architecture decision records, post-incident reviews, regulatory guidance documents), automated indexing and embedding pipelines that update the vector database as knowledge assets change, relevance scoring that ensures agents retrieve the most pertinent context, and provenance tracking that records which knowledge sources influenced each agent recommendation. The vector database is partitioned by sovereign zone where knowledge assets contain zone-specific information, ensuring that agents operating in one zone do not inadvertently access or disclose information from another.

In the fully agentic enterprise, knowledge management is bidirectional: agents consume knowledge to inform their reasoning, and they contribute to knowledge by capturing insights from incidents, changes, and operational patterns — creating a self-improving knowledge base that evolves with the estate. A post-incident review that reveals a new failure pattern is automatically indexed and available to agents handling future incidents. A successful remediation that deviates from the standard runbook is captured as a knowledge contribution and flagged for human review to determine whether the runbook should be updated. This bidirectional flow is the mechanism through which the organisation's operational intelligence compounds over time.

# Part VII — AI Governance and Explainability

## Chapter 21 — AI Risks in Operational Contexts

Deploying AI agents in production operations introduces risks qualitatively different from traditional automation. Traditional automation executes deterministic logic: given the same inputs, it produces the same outputs, and the failure modes are predictable and testable. AI agents operate probabilistically, and their behaviour may vary with context, training data, model updates, and the specific combination of signals they encounter. The failure modes are less predictable, harder to test exhaustively, and potentially more consequential because agents may act with misplaced confidence.

The chapter catalogues five principal risk categories. First, *recommendation errors*: a misdiagnosed root cause that sends engineers on a false trail while the actual problem escalates, or a remediation recommendation that addresses a symptom while the underlying cause continues to degrade the system. Second, *hallucinated diagnostics*: confident but fabricated explanations that are plausible enough to consume investigation time — an agent that attributes a latency spike to a database query pattern that does not actually exist. Third, *scope violations*: agents taking actions outside their defined authority, either through misconfigured guardrails or through reasoning chains that progressively expand the agent's self-perceived mandate. Fourth, *bias in reasoning*: systematically favouring certain remediation patterns (for example, always recommending service restarts rather than investigating configuration issues) because those patterns are over-represented in the training data or the operational history. Fifth, *adversarial manipulation*: prompt injection that alters an agent's behaviour, data poisoning that corrupts the knowledge base agents rely upon, or signal manipulation that feeds false telemetry to trick an agent into taking harmful actions.

Each risk is paired with specific mitigations: independent validation of high-stakes recommendations by a separate agent or human, mandatory confidence calibration (agents must distinguish between high-confidence and uncertain assessments and escalate the latter), explicit scope boundaries enforced through policy-as-code, diversity in agent architectures and foundation models to avoid correlated failures, and input validation and anomaly detection on agent behaviour itself — monitoring the monitors. The overarching principle is that AI risk management in operations is not a separate discipline but an extension of operational risk management, adapted for probabilistic, learning systems.

## Chapter 22 — watsonx.governance as a Governance Platform

watsonx.governance provides the practical platform for AI governance in sovereign operations. It maintains a model inventory — a comprehensive registry of every AI model and agent deployed in the estate, including its version, its training data provenance (which datasets were used, from which zones, with what data governance approvals), its performance metrics (accuracy, latency, confidence distribution), its approval status (which governance body approved it, when, with what conditions), and its sovereign zone deployments (where it runs, what data it can access, what actions it can take). No model or agent is deployed in the production estate without being registered in the inventory and having passed through the defined governance lifecycle.

Lifecycle governance ensures that models progress through defined stages — development, validation, staging, production deployment, monitoring, and retirement — with approval gates at each transition. The validation stage includes testing against representative data from the target sovereign zone, performance benchmarking against defined acceptance criteria, adversarial testing to probe for known vulnerability patterns, and review by the AI Governance Board or its delegated authority. No model enters production without having passed through this pipeline, and the evidence from each stage is retained as part of the model's governance record.

Performance monitoring is continuous: watsonx.governance tracks model accuracy, drift (changes in performance over time as the operational data distribution shifts — a model trained on summer traffic patterns may perform differently in December), fairness metrics (ensuring that recommendations do not systematically disadvantage certain service categories or zones), and operational performance (inference latency, token consumption, error rates). When metrics fall below defined thresholds, the platform alerts governance bodies and can automatically restrict the model to advisory-only mode — Level 2 autonomy — until the issue is investigated and resolved. This continuous assurance satisfies the EU AI Act's post-market monitoring requirements (Article 72) and provides the evidence base for the quarterly governance reviews that calibrate agent autonomy boundaries.

The platform manages the documentation requirements of the EU AI Act. Technical documentation (Article 11), records of model behaviour (Article 12), and transparency information (Article 13) are generated and maintained as part of the normal model lifecycle rather than as retrospective compliance exercises. When a regulator asks "how do you govern the AI systems in your operational estate?", the answer is not a policy document but a live demonstration of the governance platform: the model inventory, the lifecycle records, the continuous performance monitoring, and the structured evidence that every model in production has been validated, approved, and is being monitored — all within sovereign zone boundaries.

## Chapter 23 — The Sovereign AI Record

The sovereign AI record is the evidentiary backbone of AI governance in sovereign operations. It is a structured, immutable, time-series log of every consequential agent action: what situation triggered the action, what model was invoked, what context was provided, what recommendation was generated, what confidence score was assigned, what policy evaluated the recommendation, what human (if any) approved it, what automation executed it, and what the verified outcome was.

The sovereign AI record satisfies multiple regulatory requirements simultaneously. DORA requires that financial entities maintain comprehensive records of ICT-related incidents and their management (Article 17). The EU AI Act requires that high-risk AI systems produce logs enabling monitoring of their operation (Article 12) and post-market surveillance (Article 72). GDPR requires that automated decisions affecting individuals be explainable (Article 22). The sovereign AI record provides a single evidentiary source that addresses all three frameworks, capturing the complete decision chain from signal to action to outcome.

The record is stored within sovereign zone boundaries with the same data residency controls as any other regulated data. Cross-zone queries operate through filtered aggregate patterns: zone-local records are queried locally, and only anonymised, aggregated results are returned to the requesting entity. For post-incident review and regulatory reporting, the sovereign AI record provides a complete, timestamped account of everything that happened — eliminating the unreliable, time-consuming process of reconstructing timelines from chat logs, emails, and the memories of the engineers who were on call.

## Chapter 29 — Autonomous Self-Healing Systems

Self-healing represents the higher end of the autonomy spectrum for sovereign operations. The chapter maps five levels of operational autonomy, adapted from the SAE autonomy levels used in autonomous vehicles [7]. At **Level 0** (fully manual), humans perform all detection, diagnosis, and remediation. At **Level 1** (tool-assisted), tools surface information but humans make all decisions. At **Level 2** (partially automated), specific remediations are automated but require human approval. At **Level 3** (conditionally autonomous), the system detects, diagnoses, and executes remediation without human pre-approval for a defined set of conditions, action types, and environments — this is where self-healing, as commonly understood, begins. At **Level 4** (highly autonomous), the system handles the overwhelming majority of events autonomously, with human involvement reserved for unprecedented situations. At **Level 5** (fully autonomous), no human involvement is required.

The book argues that no responsible sovereign operations architecture should target Level 5 for regulated workloads. DORA, the EU AI Act, and sector-specific frameworks universally require demonstrable human accountability for consequential operational decisions. The practical target is Level 3 for most operational domains, advancing to Level 4 selectively for well-understood, low-risk, high-frequency tasks.

The **autonomous action boundary** governs which actions agents may take without human approval, defined as a classification matrix of three dimensions: *reversibility* (can the action be undone? a pod restart is fully reversible; a database schema migration is not), *blast radius* (how many services, users, or zones are affected? a single container restart has minimal blast radius; a network policy change may affect thousands of connections), and *regulatory sensitivity* (does the action touch regulated data or critical infrastructure? actions in development zones have lower sensitivity than actions in production zones governed by DORA). This matrix is encoded as policy-as-code and evaluated at execution time.

**Progressive autonomy** is the model through which agents earn wider boundaries. An agent begins at Level 2 — proposing actions for human approval. As its track record builds, measured by recommendation accuracy, success rate, false positive rate, and absence of harmful outcomes, its autonomy boundary is widened through a formal governance process. Promotion criteria are quantitative: a minimum number of successful recommendations, a maximum error rate, a minimum operating period. Demotion triggers are asymmetric: a single harmful outcome triggers immediate boundary reduction, reflecting the operational reality that a single bad autonomous action can cause more damage than many good ones can accumulate benefit.

Safety mechanisms are layered as defence in depth: rate limiting (maximum actions per time window), blast-radius containment (bounded scope per action), automatic rollback (health monitoring with reversal if metrics deteriorate), dead man's switch (agents must continuously affirm operational status; silence triggers escalation), and escalation cascades (situations exceeding competency reach a human with appropriate authority within defined time bounds). The chapter also addresses cyber-resilient recovery patterns: IBM Safeguarded Copy for immutable, zone-local snapshots; cyber vault architectures for isolated, hardened backup environments; and clean room recovery for restoring workloads into validated infrastructure — integrated with agentic orchestration and aligned to DORA Article 12 backup and recovery requirements.

## Chapter 30 — Agent-Assisted Incident Management

The full incident lifecycle — detection, triage, diagnosis, remediation, communication, post-incident review — is examined through the lens of agent assistance. At each stage, agents contribute specific capabilities while humans retain specific responsibilities.

**Detection** is agent-driven: Instana's anomaly detection identifies deviations from learned baselines within seconds, and Concert's correlation engine maps the anomaly to the topology model to assess scope and impact. # Part X — Operating Model and Human Factors

## Chapter 31 — The Human Operating Model

The introduction of agentic capabilities requires corresponding changes to the human operating model — changes that are organisational and cultural, not merely technical. The book argues that the operating model transformation is at least as challenging as the technology deployment, and that organisations that invest in technology without transforming their operating model achieve the tooling-only transformation anti-pattern identified in Chapter 33: new tools used in old ways, with minimal improvement in outcomes.

New roles emerge. The **agent governance lead** is responsible for defining and maintaining agent autonomy boundaries, reviewing agent performance data from the sovereign AI record, conducting quarterly agent performance reviews, and making promotion and demotion recommendations to the AI Governance Board. The **prompt engineer** crafts and refines the instructions, guardrails, and context that shape agent behaviour — a role that requires both technical depth (understanding what the agent can and should do) and operational judgement (understanding the consequences of agent actions in production). The **sovereign zone operator** is responsible for the operational integrity of a specific zone, including its observability coverage, compliance posture, inter-zone interfaces, and the zone-specific autonomy policies that govern agent behaviour within the zone.

Existing roles evolve significantly. The SRE shifts from primarily reactive incident response — being woken at three in the morning to diagnose a problem manually — to primarily proactive work: designing agent guardrails, defining SLOs, reviewing agent-handled incidents for learning opportunities, and improving the automation library. The compliance officer shifts from periodic evidence assembly (the dreaded audit preparation season) to continuous posture review, working with the compliance agent to prioritise remediation and refine policy-as-code definitions based on real operational experience.

Decision rights must be explicitly defined. The book provides a decision rights framework that maps decision types to authority levels: which decisions can agents make autonomously (routine, low-risk, reversible actions within the autonomous action boundary), which require human approval (high-risk, irreversible, or regulatory-sensitive actions), and which require escalation to governance bodies (changes to autonomy boundaries, introduction of new agent types, changes to sovereign zone policies). The framework is informed by the autonomous action boundary and operationalised through the policy-as-code definitions that the governance plane enforces.

The operating model must also address the transition period. During the months or years of migration from traditional to agentic operations, teams must maintain manual operational capability while building and learning to trust agentic capabilities. The chapter provides guidance on phased transition, including the practice of running agents in advisory mode (Level 2) alongside human operators to build the track record that justifies autonomy boundary expansion, and the organisational structures — including dedicated "agentic operations" teams that operate alongside traditional teams during the transition — that sustain both modes simultaneously. The transition is not a switch to be flipped; it is a gradual shift in the distribution of work between humans and agents, governed by evidence and calibrated by experience.

## Chapter 32 — Skills, Culture, and Human Factors

Technology investments translate into operational outcomes only through the people who use and govern them. The chapter addresses three dimensions. First, **skills development**: agentic operations requires specific skills — prompt engineering for effective agent interaction, policy-as-code authorship in Rego for sovereignty enforcement, agent behaviour evaluation using the metrics captured in the sovereign AI record, and observability data analysis using the five-signal model. The chapter provides guidance on building these skills through training, rotation across operational roles, and deliberate practice in controlled environments.

Second, **cultural readiness**: the shift from blame culture to blameless post-incident review is a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have. In a blame culture, agents' mistakes become ammunition for those who opposed agentic operations; in a blameless culture, they become learning opportunities that improve agent behaviour and governance. The shift from knowledge hoarding to systematic knowledge sharing is essential because agents depend on the knowledge base that humans curate. The shift from hero-driven operations (where individual heroics save the day) to system-driven operations (where well-designed systems handle routine challenges reliably) is the cultural foundation on which agentic operations rests.

Third, **trust calibration**: engineers must develop appropriate trust in agentic systems — neither blind faith that leads to over-reliance nor reflexive distrust that prevents agents from delivering value. Trust is calibrated through transparency (every agent action is inspectable), track record (quantitative performance data builds evidence-based confidence), and deliberate exercises in which engineers operate without agent assistance to maintain their manual skills and their independent judgement.

The chapter draws on Westrum's typology of organisational cultures [8] to argue that the cultural prerequisites for effective agentic operations — high cooperation, blameless learning, information sharing, novelty seeking — are the same characteristics that predict high performance in traditional engineering organisations. Agentic operations does not require a fundamentally different culture; it requires the culture that the best engineering organisations already aspire to, with the additional dimension of human-agent partnership.

## Chapter 33 — The Sovereign Operations Maturity Model

The maturity model provides the assessment framework that connects the book's architectural prescriptions to the organisation's actual operational state. It is purpose-built for sovereign, agentic operations, filling a gap left by CMMI (which does not address sovereignty), the DORA metrics (which measure outcomes but not operational capability), and the CNCF Cloud Native Maturity Model (which does not address policy-as-code enforcement or agent governance).

**Five maturity levels** define the progression. **Level 1 — Ad-hoc and Reactive**: operations driven by individual effort, infrastructure manually provisioned, observability limited to basic monitoring, incidents diagnosed through console access and tribal knowledge, no meaningful automation, sovereignty defined only in policy documents. **Level 2 — Managed and Repeatable**: basic discipline established — IaC for some environments, structured logging for major services, codified runbooks for common tasks, sovereign zones defined and basic region restrictions applied, agents absent or in ungoverned experimentation. **Level 3 — Defined and Standardised**: practices consistent across the organisation — IaC is the standard deployment mechanism with peer review and automated testing, observability covers the full MELT stack with cross-signal correlation, sovereign zones enforced through policy-as-code with continuous evaluation, agents operating in advisory roles with explicit guardrails. **Level 4 — Measured and Integrated**: quantitative data drives decisions — SLO attainment tracked and analysed, change intelligence connects deployments to outcomes, sovereignty enforcement continuous and auditable with drift detection, agents performing bounded operational actions under policy governance, cross-functional integration deep. **Level 5 — Optimised and Autonomous**: practices self-improving — feedback loops refine runbooks, policies, and agent behaviour; agents manage well-defined domains with genuine bounded autonomy; sovereignty is a platform property applying automatically to new services; knowledge management is active and bidirectional.

**Eight assessment dimensions** provide the measurement axes: **infrastructure codification**, **observability depth**, **automation coverage**, **sovereignty enforcement**, **agent adoption**, **governance maturity**, **cultural readiness**, and **knowledge management**. An organisation's maturity is not a single number but a profile across these dimensions, and the unevenness of the profile is often more revealing than the average. An organisation with Level 4 observability but Level 1 agent governance has a clear investment priority.

Critical interdependencies constrain the advancement path. Agent adoption depends on observability depth and automation coverage — an agent that cannot observe cannot decide, and an agent that has no automation to invoke cannot act. Sovereignty enforcement depends on infrastructure codification — policy-as-code cannot evaluate infrastructure that is not defined in code. Governance maturity depends on cultural readiness — governance imposed on a blame culture becomes compliance theatre.

The chapter recommends combining **quick wins** that build credibility within weeks (introducing observability for a critical service, automating a recurring incident's remediation, generating compliance reports from telemetry) with **structural moves** that reshape foundations over quarters. The **sovereign core pattern** is the recommended structural approach: define a new sovereign zone with full constraints (IaC, comprehensive observability, policy-as-code, zero-copy integration), move a carefully chosen set of services into it, and build governed interfaces between the core and the legacy estate. The core demonstrates the target state at small scale; the interfaces ensure coexistence and gradual absorption of legacy workloads.

Five transformation anti-patterns are identified: the **big-bang rewrite** (replace everything at once — by the time it is ready, the world has changed), the **new silo** (a perfect platform that only new services use while critical workloads remain in the legacy estate), the **tooling-only transformation** (buy tools without changing operating models — the new tools are used like the old ones), **paper-only sovereignty** (policies written that cannot be technically enforced), and the **unbounded agent** (broad production access without guardrails — when it misbehaves, trust in the entire agentic approach collapses).

Progress is sustained through quarterly maturity reviews, leading indicators (percentage of new infrastructure via IaC, mean time to root cause, policy violation count, agent recommendation acceptance rate), and explicit connection to business outcomes (reduced MTTR translates to revenue preserved, automated evidence translates to reduced audit cost, improved change failure rate translates to engineering time freed for value-creating work). The model serves operations, not the other way around.

# Part XI — Blueprints, Patterns, and Case Studies

## Chapters 34–35 — Reference Blueprints and Industry Patterns

The blueprint chapters translate the architectural reference model into concrete, deployable patterns for regulated contexts. Each blueprint specifies the sovereign zone topology, the technology components mapped to the four planes, the policy-as-code framework with specific regulatory article mappings, the observability architecture with zone-aware data handling, the agentic capabilities with defined autonomy boundaries, and the governance structure appropriate for the sector's regulatory environment. The blueprints are not abstract architectures; they are intended to serve as starting points for implementation, with sufficient specificity that an architecture team can use them to scope a programme and estimate effort.

The **regulated financial services blueprint** addresses a multi-entity banking group operating across EU jurisdictions. Sovereign zones are aligned to national regulatory authorities — BaFin (Germany), ACPR (France), DNB (Netherlands) — with an EU parent zone defining DORA and GDPR baseline controls that child zones inherit. Concert operates as a group-wide reasoning layer, consuming filtered aggregates from zone-local Instana backends while raw telemetry remains within jurisdictional boundaries. Policy-as-code enforces DORA Article 9 risk management controls (continuous risk assessment of ICT systems), Article 11 response and recovery obligations (tested backup and restoration procedures), Article 12 backup requirements (geographically diverse backup with sovereign zone alignment), and Article 28 third-party risk requirements (continuous monitoring of cloud provider SLAs and compliance posture). Zero-copy integration replaces batch replication, with zone-aware event streaming providing near-real-time data consistency across entities while maintaining strict data residency. The blueprint includes integration patterns for core banking, payments, risk calculation, and regulatory reporting, with specific attention to the DORA Article 19 incident reporting workflow — where Concert's situation assessment, structured with the required classification criteria, feeds directly into the mandatory four-hour initial notification to the competent authority.

The **national public sector blueprint** addresses a government digital services platform with varying security classifications. Sovereign zones are aligned to data classification levels — public, official, sensitive, secret — with corresponding controls for data handling, access, network isolation, and personnel clearance requirements. The blueprint addresses legacy system integration, where mainframe and mid-range systems coexist with containerised microservices and require translation layers for event-driven integration. It also addresses the particular governance challenge of the public sector: multi-departmental estates where each department has historical autonomy over its technology choices but must now operate within a common sovereign framework that satisfies NIS2 requirements for government entities. The blueprint includes patterns for citizen data protection, including pseudonymisation at zone boundaries and consent-based access controls for cross-departmental data sharing.

The **healthcare blueprint** addresses a national health service or hospital network operating under GDPR, the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR), and national health data protection legislation. Sovereign zones are aligned to clinical data sensitivity, with the most restrictive zones governing patient-identifiable health records and clinical decision support systems. The blueprint includes patterns for clinical AI governance, where AI-assisted operational recommendations (such as the bed management agents in Case Study B) must satisfy both the EU AI Act's requirements for high-risk systems and the MDR's requirements for software that may influence clinical decisions. The boundary between operational AI (which recommends bed allocation and discharge coordination) and clinical AI (which influences diagnosis or treatment) must be carefully maintained to avoid unintended regulatory classification as a medical device.

Additional industry patterns extend these to insurance, telecommunications, energy and utilities, and manufacturing, each with sector-specific regulatory mappings. The energy and utilities pattern addresses the IEC 62443 security level framework for operational technology environments, with air-gapped OT zones, governed IT/OT integration points, and the specific constraints of managing control systems where safety and availability take absolute precedence over all other operational concerns.

## Chapter 36 — Case Studies

Five composite case studies, drawn from patterns observed across real organisations, ground the architectural framework in operational reality with quantitative outcomes.

**Case Study A: European Banking Group.** A mid-tier banking group with retail and corporate operations across Germany, France, the Netherlands, and the UK, employing approximately 3,200 technology staff. The challenge: DORA compliance requiring group-wide ICT risk management; three separate monitoring suites generating over 140,000 alerts per month with no cross-subsidiary correlation; and 340 identified replicated datasets creating unmanageable data residency risk. The programme defined sovereign zones at jurisdictional level (implemented as OpenShift clusters with zone-local identity and observability), replaced batch replication with zero-copy event streaming through zone-aware Kafka topic routing, and deployed Concert as a group-wide reasoning layer consuming filtered aggregates from zone-local Instana backends. **Outcomes after eighteen months**: MTTD reduced from 23 minutes to under 4 minutes; MTTR reduced by 62 per cent; 140,000 monthly alerts consolidated into 4,200 correlated situations; data copies reduced from 340 to 41 under exception management; DORA readiness from 34 to 89 per cent of controls evidenced through continuous automation; annual audit preparation reduced by 2,100 person-hours.

**Case Study B: National Health Service.** A health service across twenty million people with national EHR, regional hospital systems, and imaging archive. Cross-system MTTD was eleven hours; hospital bed occupancy was 94 per cent with delayed discharges contributing to bed shortages. The programme implemented sovereign observability, replaced nightly batch reconciliation with event-driven synchronisation (eliminating "silent reconciliation failure"), and introduced bed management agents that consumed streams of hospital events and generated discharge coordination recommendations under strict clinical guardrails. **Outcomes after two years**: cross-system MTTD from eleven hours to eighteen minutes; bed occupancy from 94 to 87 per cent; agent discharge recommendations accepted by clinical staff in 71 per cent of cases; two months of continuous compliance evidence available at all times versus the previous annual audit snapshot.

**Case Study C: Pan-European Insurer.** Operating across five jurisdictions with claims, policy administration, and customer portal. Moved from monolithic claims platform to event-driven architecture with sovereign zones. Agent-assisted claims triage reduced initial assessment time by 40 per cent. Compliance evidence generation shifted from six-week annual exercises to continuous automated production.

**Case Study D: Government Digital Services.** A national platform with varying security classifications. Implemented classification-aligned sovereign zones, progressive observability, and agent-assisted incident management. Citizen-facing availability improved from 99.2 to 99.8 per cent. Priority-one MTTR from 4.2 hours to 47 minutes.

**Case Study E: Critical National Infrastructure.** An energy transmission operator across OT and IT domains. Implemented IEC 62443-aligned sovereign zones with air-gapped OT zones and governed integration points. Agent-assisted anomaly detection achieved 94 per cent detection rate for simulated attack scenarios during resilience testing.

Cross-cutting lessons: observability must precede automation; zero-copy integration is a sovereignty enabler; policy-as-code requires iterative refinement under real operating conditions; organisational and cultural change is at least as demanding as the technical migration.

# Part XII — The Horizon

## Chapter 37 — Generative AI and the Near-Term Evolution

The penultimate chapter examines the near-term trajectory of generative AI in sovereign operations over the next two to five years. It distinguishes between capabilities that are production-ready today — signal correlation, recommendation generation, runbook automation, conversational operations, advisory agents — and capabilities that are emerging but not yet mature — autonomous root cause analysis for novel failure modes, cross-domain reasoning that connects operational signals to business impact and customer experience, self-improving agent behaviour that refines its own procedures based on outcome data, and agents that can design and propose architectural changes based on observed operational patterns.

The trajectory is one of progressive deepening rather than revolutionary discontinuity. Each generation of foundation model improvement translates into more accurate correlation, more nuanced reasoning, and wider applicability of autonomous action — but the architectural framework remains stable. The four-planes model, the sovereign zone design, the policy-as-code governance, and the sovereign AI record do not change when a better model is deployed; they accommodate better models within the same governed boundaries.

The chapter addresses three specific challenges that generative AI introduces for sovereign operations. First, **model governance at operational scale**: when foundation models are updated — whether by the model vendor or through fine-tuning on operational data — how does the organisation ensure that agent behaviour in production does not degrade? The answer involves shadow evaluation: running the updated model alongside the current model in a non-production pathway, comparing recommendations on identical inputs, and promoting only when the updated model meets or exceeds the current model's performance across all relevant metrics. This shadow evaluation is integrated with watsonx.governance's lifecycle management, providing a governed promotion pathway that satisfies the EU AI Act's post-market monitoring requirements.

Second, **data sovereignty for inference**: where does the model run (on-premises, in a specific cloud region, at the edge?), where does the context data flow during inference, and do those locations satisfy zone constraints? For regulated workloads, inference must occur within the sovereign zone — a requirement that has significant implications for model deployment architecture and GPU capacity planning. A financial services firm operating under DORA cannot send operational context from its German sovereign zone to a model running in a US-hosted inference service, regardless of how capable that service might be. The architecture must support zone-local inference, which in turn requires investment in zone-local GPU infrastructure or the use of smaller, efficient models that can run on available compute.

Third, **the evolving regulatory landscape**: the EU AI Act's implementing regulations, the AI Office's guidance documents, national AI strategies, sector-specific supervisory positions, and emerging international standards will continue to refine the constraints on operational AI over the coming years. The sovereign operations architecture must be designed to accommodate regulatory evolution without requiring architectural redesign — which is precisely why the governance plane is a separate architectural concern with its own policy-as-code framework, rather than being embedded in the agents themselves.

## Chapter 38 — The Fully Agentic Enterprise

The closing chapter describes the mature state of agentic operations: an enterprise in which AI agents participate persistently across every significant operational process as capable, bounded, supervised colleagues operating predominantly at autonomy Levels 3 and 4. The phrase "fully agentic" does not mean fully autonomous. It describes an enterprise in which agents are woven into the operational fabric — not waiting to be invoked during crises but continuously observing, correlating, recommending, and, within governed boundaries, acting. Humans remain accountable. Sovereignty remains non-negotiable. But the texture of daily operations and the distribution of cognitive labour between people and machines are qualitatively different.

The chapter grounds the vision in a detailed **day-in-the-life narrative set in 2030** for a European financial services firm operating across three sovereign zones on two hyperscaler providers and an on-premises private cloud. At 06:15, the operations lead reviews the overnight summary: fourteen events handled autonomously, including twelve routine tasks (certificate rotations, capacity adjustments, transient network mitigations) and two significant remediations (a memory leak diagnosed as a deployment regression and remediated by automatic rollback within the autonomous action boundary). Review time: twelve minutes. Under the previous model, two overnight pages and forty minutes each. At 09:30, a change risk agent produces a structured assessment of a planned hyperscaler migration, identifying a hard dependency, a 4ms latency increase, and a brief sovereign zone boundary risk during cutover — detailed narrative with evidence, not a traffic-light score. At 11:00, the compliance agent's weekly report, generated from continuous policy evaluation, identifies a test service in the EU zone referencing an external API outside the EU, proposes two remediation paths, and has already raised a ticket citing the specific policy clause. At 14:00, a genuine incident — certificate propagation failure causing payment service errors — is detected in seconds, diagnosed in ninety seconds, remediated with human approval in four minutes, and documented automatically. At 16:30, the capacity planning agent presents a quarterly forecast, projecting a 20 per cent database capacity increase needed within eight weeks, and translates the approved plan into Terraform changes.

The **trust equation at maturity** has four terms: **transparency** (every action recorded and auditable through the sovereign AI record), **track record** (quantitative performance data for every agent class building Bayesian confidence), **governance** (the AI Governance Board reviewing performance quarterly, making documented boundary decisions), and **humility** (agents that know their limits and escalate honestly when situations exceed their competence — an agent that confidently proposes a remediation for an unprecedented situation is more dangerous than one that escalates with an honest assessment of its uncertainty).

The chapter confronts **five risks** honestly. **Over-reliance** erodes the intuitive system understanding that comes from direct engagement — the same phenomenon documented in aviation automation [9], where automation improved safety overall but created instances where pilots lacked manual skills for situations automation could not handle. **Skills atrophy** extends to the broader organisation as agents compose Terraform, draft runbooks, and generate compliance reports. **Correlated agent failures** arise when shared foundation models have shared blind spots. **Adversarial attacks** target agents as high-value assets. **The accountability gap** — when recommendation, approval, execution, and harm form a chain involving humans and machines, the allocation of accountability remains philosophically and legally unsettled, though frameworks are clear that human accountability cannot be delegated.

Against these risks, the **sovereignty dividend** accrues to organisations that build this capability well. **Optionality**: genuine choice about providers, unconstrained by proprietary entanglements. **Resilience**: faster detection, diagnosis, remediation — MTTD in seconds, root cause in under a minute, governed remediation in under five minutes. **Speed**: deployment frequency, lead time, and time to restore improve when compliance and risk assessment are embedded in the flow. **Cost efficiency**: engineering expertise reallocated from routine correlation to architectural improvement. **Regulatory confidence**: on-demand, complete, auditable records of how the estate is governed, how agents operate, and how every consequential action was authorised — producing a quality of regulatory relationship that retrospective documentation assembly cannot match.

The closing reflection returns to the book's design principle: *build systems that make it easier for good engineers to do the right thing under pressure*. The fully agentic enterprise is not a destination but a sustainable practice — tending an evolving estate with care, rigour, and enough slack that people can think. Whether the tools carry IBM's name or another's, the patterns endure: sovereign zones enforcing boundaries, zero-copy integration reducing governance surface, policy-as-code making compliance continuous, observability understanding topology, agents recommending and acting within governed boundaries, governance producing evidence, and humans who design, supervise, improve, and remain — always — the accountable parties.
